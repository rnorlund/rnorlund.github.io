About

I'm a cognitive neuroscientist at the University of South Carolina, where I direct the USC Aging Brain Cohort (ABC) Study and serve as a core investigator at the South Carolina Alzheimer's Disease Research Center (SC-ADRC). I'm also a Research Associate Professor in Communication Sciences and Disorders at the Arnold School of Public Health.

My work sits at the intersection of brain imaging, machine learning, and rehabilitation — trying to understand why some brains age gracefully while others don't, and how we can use that knowledge to help people recover from stroke and language disorders.

I earned my PhD in Cognitive Neuroscience from Dartmouth College (2005), spent four years as a postdoc at the Donders Institute in the Netherlands, and have been at USC since 2009. One of my favourite discoveries: the mirror neuron system fires more strongly when you complement someone's action than when you simply imitate them (Nature Neuroscience, 2007).

Research

Structural & functional MRI Healthy aging Post-stroke aphasia Brain age estimation Mirror neuron system Motor learning & control Deep learning / CNNs tDCS & TMS Alzheimer's disease Teletherapy Joint action Second language acquisition White matter hyperintensities Cardiovascular & brain health

My lab uses state-of-the-art MRI and machine learning to figure out how the brain changes across the lifespan — and what those changes mean for language, memory, and recovery after stroke. We're particularly interested in how cardiovascular health, white matter integrity, and brain age relate to cognitive function in both healthy aging and post-stroke populations.
