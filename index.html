About

I am a neuroscientist and the Vice President for Research at the University of South Carolina (USC), where I also hold the SmartState Endowed Chair in Brain and Memory Function and serve as a Health Sciences Distinguished Professor in the Arnold School of Public Health. I joined the USC faculty in 2001 and have dedicated my career to understanding how the human brain processes language and how it recovers following stroke-induced injury.

Born in Keflavík, Iceland, and the first in my family to graduate from college, I earned my Ph.D. in Speech and Hearing Sciences from the University of Arizona in 2001. My research has been supported by more than $50 million in NIH funding, and I am recognized as the most-cited researcher worldwide on aphasia — a language disorder affecting millions of stroke survivors.

Beyond the lab, I co-founded ALLT.AI, a USC spin-off applying insights from brain lesion research to improve the energy efficiency of large language models (LLMs) — bridging neuroscience and artificial intelligence.

Research

Aphasia & stroke recovery Neuroimaging (fMRI / MRI) Brain stimulation (tDCS) Lesion-symptom mapping Neurobiology of language Cognitive neuroscience Machine learning & AI COVID-19 & brain health

My lab uses neuroimaging, brain stimulation, and computational methods to map the neural architecture of language and to develop evidence-based rehabilitation strategies for people with aphasia. A central goal is translating findings from the bench to the bedside — from understanding which brain regions support syntax and naming, to testing whether transcranial direct current stimulation can accelerate recovery after stroke.
