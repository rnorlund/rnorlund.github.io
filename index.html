About Me

I'm a cognitive neuroscientist at the University of South Carolina, where I direct the USC Aging Brain Cohort (ABC) Study and serve as Managing Director of the McCausland Center for Brain Imaging. I'm also a core investigator at the South Carolina Alzheimer's Disease Research Center (SC-ADRC) and a Research Associate Professor in Communication Sciences and Disorders.

My work sits at the intersection of brain imaging, machine learning, and rehabilitation — trying to understand why some brains age gracefully while others don't, and how we can use that knowledge to help people recover from stroke and language disorders.

I earned my PhD in Cognitive Neuroscience from Dartmouth College in 2005, spent four years as a postdoc at the Donders Institute in the Netherlands, and relocated to Columbia, SC in 2009 with my wife Sarah (who also works in the lab — handy!).

Research

Structural & functional MRI Healthy aging Post-stroke aphasia Brain age estimation Mirror neuron system Motor learning & control Deep learning / CNNs tDCS & TMS Alzheimer's disease Teletherapy Joint action

My lab uses state-of-the-art MRI and machine learning to figure out how the brain changes across the lifespan — and what those changes mean for language, memory, and recovery after stroke. One of my favourite discoveries: the mirror neuron system fires more strongly when you complement someone's action than when you simply imitate them (Nature Neuroscience, 2007). Brains are social organs.

USC Aging Brain Cohort participants — tracking how our brains change over time.
